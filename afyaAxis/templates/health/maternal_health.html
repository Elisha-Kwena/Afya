MATERNAL AND CHILD CARE

Maternal and Child Care refers to a comprehensive approach to healthcare that focuses on ensuring the health and well-being of mothers and their children. It involves a wide range of services and practices that support women during pregnancy, childbirth, and the postnatal period, as well as the health and development of children from birth through adolescence.

Maternal Health Care

Maternal healthcare refers to the medical care provided to women during pregnancy, childbirth, and postpartum. It aims to ensure a healthy pregnancy, safe delivery, and proper postpartum recovery.

  • Preconception Care
    Ensures women are in optimal health before pregnancy.
    Includes folic acid supplementation, vaccinations, and management of pre-existing conditions.
  • Antenatal (Prenatal) Care
    Regular check-ups to monitor fetal development and maternal health.
    Includes ultrasound scans, blood tests, and screening for complications (e.g., gestational diabetes, preeclampsia).
    Nutrition counseling and supplementation (iron, calcium, and folic acid).

  • Labor and Delivery Care
    Monitoring for normal and high-risk deliveries.
    Availability of skilled birth attendants.
    Emergency obstetric care (C-sections, treatment of hemorrhage, and infections).

Common Maternal Health Issues

  1. Anemia: Common due to iron deficiency during pregnancy.
  2. Gestational Diabetes: Affects blood sugar levels and requires monitoring.
  3. Preeclampsia: High blood pressure during pregnancy that can be life-threatening.
  4. Postpartum Depression: Affects mental health and requires early intervention.
  5. Obstetric Fistula: A serious childbirth-related injury causing urinary incontinence.

CHILD HEALTH

Child health focuses on ensuring children grow up healthy, with proper nutrition, immunization, and medical care.

A. Newborn and Infant Care (0-12 months)

  1. Immediate care after birth (cord care, skin-to-skin contact, early breastfeeding).
  2. Screening for birth defects and infections.
  3. Prevention of neonatal jaundice, hypothermia, and infections.
  4. Exclusive breastfeeding for the first six months.
  5. Introduction of solid foods after six months (complementary feeding).
  6. Vitamin A, iron, and zinc supplementation if needed.
  7. Essential vaccines: BCG, Hepatitis B, Polio, DPT (Diphtheria, Pertussis, Tetanus), Measles, Rotavirus.

B. Early Childhood Care (1-5 years)

  1. Monitoring weight, height, and cognitive development.
  2. Preventing malnutrition and micronutrient deficiencies.
  3. Diarrhea (prevented by good hygiene and ORS treatment).
  4. Pneumonia (prevented by vaccines and early treatment).
  5. Malaria (prevented by mosquito nets and medication).
  6. Measles (prevented by vaccination).

C. School-Age Child Health (6-12 years)

  1. Continued nutrition and balanced diet.
  2. Physical activity to prevent obesity and other health issues.
  3. Vision and hearing screenings.
  4. Prevention of infections through hygiene education.
